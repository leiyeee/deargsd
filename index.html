First open letter from Mdes student Ran Mei
10/26/2020

>>Dear Students of the MDes Program

I am writing this brief note just to clear up what we see as few failures of messaging about the reconfiguration of the MDes program.
The current Areas of Concentration are defined by two things: required courses in the respective discourses and methodologies (we tend to call them “proseminars”) and the subsequent organization of each individual student’s courses. Fundamentally the Areas are paths through the curriculum. The path through the curriculum is what the reorganization calls “Trajectories.” In short, Trajectories are exactly analogous to the Areas. What is more, all the names of the present Areas can still, in principle, be deployed using the new system. Perhaps we should have emphasized that more. A top priority of the change was to enable MDes students more autonomy in determining their areas of study or Trajectories.
In order to enable this expansion of the number of Trajectories, we provided four basic Domains of knowledge from which the Trajectories are launched. These are foundations, which, as the present program, provide knowledge about discourses and methodologies common across specializations. They are not degree areas. In fact, the required courses in each Domain are few, probably three, to be taken in the first year. But please note that, unlike in the present system, all Trajectories, by virtue of being launched from Domains, will now equally be provided with required foundation courses. In the previous system, areas with a small number of students did not have their own designated discourse and methods courses. And note, too, that, while different Trajectories can be launched for the same Domain, the same Trajectory can be launched from different Domains. In this way, we achieve maximum rigor plus maximum flexibility and nuance.

"Student Protest 1969..."

Printing became the protestors' most powerful tool. They published documents purloined from the deans' offices in the underground newspaper, Old Mole. Graduate School of Design students established the Strike Poster Workshop in the Great Space of Robinson Hall. They chose the red fist as the symbol of their dissent, designed silkscreens and cut stencils, and printed the fist on paper, t-shirts, and arm bands. The workshop created a variety of strike poster designs with an assortment of protest slogans, including "End ROTC," "Maybe They Can't Hear Us, Strike a Little Louder," and "12345678 STRIKE."

"Student protest 2019..."

In December of 2019, graduate students at Harvard University began strike action on Tuesday after negotiations between their union and the administration failed to reach an agreement. Several hundred members of the Harvard Graduate Students Union–United Automobile Workers (HGSU-UAW) began picketing at 10 a.m. in Harvard Yard in Cambridge, Massachusetts. Some 2,500 of the university’s more than 4,000 grad students voted in October to authorize a strike. (Kate Randall) Students holding the printed posters with the words “UAW ON STRIKE” went through the campus.
